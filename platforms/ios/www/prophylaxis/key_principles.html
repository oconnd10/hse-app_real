Prophylaxis

Key Principles of Antibiotic Prophylaxis

• Antibiotic prophylaxis is the use of antimicrobial agents to prevent infection or to prevent the clinical manifestations of infection if incubating.

• The choice of agent will be governed by the procedure and the likely potential pathogens. These guidelines apply to patients admitted from the community for clean, clean-contaminated and contaminated surgical procedures.

• Prophylactic antibiotics should be administered intravenously, prescribed in the appropriate section of the drug kardex and time of administration recorded.

• Antibiotic prophylaxis should be administered pre-operatively, ideally 30 minutes pre initial surgical incision. This ensures that blood and tissue levels are adequate at the time of the initial surgical incision. Administration of first antibiotic dose a number of hours before, during or following the procedure is not appropriate.

• If the patient has been in hospital or has a history of MRSA colonisation or infection without documented eradication, please contact the microbiologists for advice, as the patient’s antibiotic prophylaxis may need adjustment.

• An agent that may be appropriate for surgical prophylaxis may not be the optimal agent for the treatment of an established infection. Therefore, the continuation of an agent that was initially used for prophylaxis may represent suboptimal therapy.

• If in doubt, the clinical situation is complex e.g. patients with resistant organisms or patients with renal impairment or the patient is allergic to agents listed, please discuss with the microbiologist before giving prophylaxis.

MRSA screening of surgical patients – who to screen

• Patients known to be MRSA colonised and who are being re-admitted to hospital
• Patients admitted from another hospital or health-care facility (e.g., nursing home)
• Patients with non-intact skin, including wounds and ulcers
• Patients due to undergo elective high-risk surgery (e.g. vascular and orthopaedic implant surgery)
